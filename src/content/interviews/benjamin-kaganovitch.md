qa:
  - question: Ben, how did you find out about The Podcast Fellowship?
    answer: Honestly, I found it through Instagram--I was scrolling and came across
      a reel about it.  From there I got connected through my involvement in
      Jewish campus life.  I am Treasurer of the Jewish Student Association at
      UTM and will soon be joining the Hillel Toronto board as UTM Campus
      Representative.  Through those roles, I'm plugged into the broader campus
      Jewish ecosystem--Chabad, Olami, Ohr Somayach, The Maimonides Club--so
      once I came across TPF it felt like a natural fit.
  - question: Did you witness antisemitism on campus since October 7th, 2023?
    answer: >-
      UTM has a small Jewish community and a large international student
      population; the climate since October 7th has been difficult at times.  As
      a JSA executive, dealing with antisemitism on campus is something we face
      regularly: posters, stickers and hostile messaging that we constantly have
      to address.


      One particularly painful moment was when we were tabling to educate
      students about October 7th.  The table was rushed by so many people that
      security had to shut us down and ask us to leave.  We followed up
      extensively with Administration about how unacceptable that experience
      was.


      Perhaps the most striking was that on October 7th, 2025--the same day the
      JSA was holding a commemorative educational event--the UTM student union
      published a post about 'honouring our martyrs."  The student union is
      democratically elected; this post tells you something about the broader
      campus environment.  I've also seen troubling material at the St. George
      campus.


      The way I deal with it is by staying connected to community, refusing to
      be pushed away from my identity, and continuing to show up.  If anything,
      it's made me more committed, not less.
  - question: Tell us about your Jewish family background, Ben.
    answer: My family background is Ashkenazi/Litvish, primarily from Belarus.  On
      both my mother's and father's side, our roots trace back to the Gomel
      region, including the city of Rechitsa.  What makes this especially
      meaningful in my family is that my father, Dr. Albert Kaganovitch, is an
      academic historian who has actually written about Jewish life in Rechitsa
      and Eastern European Jewry more broadly.  So Jewish history isn't just
      something I learned about in school; it's something my family has actively
      studied, preserved and carried forward.  I see myself as part of that
      chain.
  - question: You most certainly are!  What was your home like growing up?   What
      Jewish education did you receive?
    answer: I grew up in a home with a strong Jewish and Israeli identity.  We knew
      our history and biblical stories, and we followed Israeli news closely,
      but we weren't observant in the traditional sense.  My formal Jewish
      education was limited when I was young.  Most of my real growth happened
      during university, where I've become much more engaged in learning,
      observance, Hebrew, and connecting with the Jewish community in a deeper
      way.
  - question: You found out about TPF on Instagram.  Would you have been interested
      in TPF without the $15 stipend offer?
    answer: Absolutely.  The stipend is appreciated, but the real value runs much
      deeper.  The guided, facilitated learning structure keeps me consistent
      with my Jewish education in a way that stand-alone self-directed study
      doesn't.  Beyond that, the networking has been genuinely impactful;
      through Rabbi Jaffit I've been connected to Jewish circles, fellowships
      and opportunities I wouln't have encountered otherwise.  The mentorship
      turns what could be just podcast consumption into something much more
      meaningful.
  - question: Tell us about your experience with TPF.
    answer: >-
      Rabbi Jaffit (Rabbi Jonathan Jaffit of [Ohr
      Campus](https://www.ohrcampus.com/theteam)) knows me well; he knows what
      I'm interested in and suggests podcasts accordingly.  I prepare by
      listening carefully, writing down questions that come up, and thinking
      about how the ideas connect to my own life.  What I've come to love about
      Jewish learning through this process is that asking questions is the
      point.


      Our conversations often go deep, sometimes extending into metaphysics and
      philosophy in ways I didn't expect.  For example, when we were discussing
      free will and G-d's omnipresence, we ended up exploring whether time
      itself is a measure of space, and what it means for G-d to exist outside
      of time.  Those kinds of conversations---rooted in Torah but genuinely
      intellectually rigorous--are what keep me coming back.
  - question: Who is your favorite podcast speaker?
    answer: My favourite speaker is Rabbi Dr. Dovid Gottleib.  He approches Jewish
      thought with real philosophical depth; his series on free will doesn't
      just present a Jewish position, it grapples seriously with the
      metaphysical complexity of the issue.  He makes you sit with hard
      questions rather than rush to easy answers, which is something I deeply
      respect.  It also connects naturally to the kinds of conversations I end
      up having with Rabbi Jaffit.
  - question: Where do you stand in your Jewish observance today?
    answer: I'm still on the journey, but I'm much more connected than I was.  I try
      to put on tefillin, I attend synagogue more regularly, I'm actively
      learning Hebrew, and I'm always looking for ways to make Judaism more
      central to my life.  I don't think of it as a destination; it's a
      continuous process, and I'm trying to move forward.
  - question: Ben, can you sum up your experience with The Podcast Fellowship in one
      quote?
    answer: “A podcast can give you ideas, but a conversation can turn those ideas
      into growth.  The more I learn about Judaism, the more I realize how much
      there still is to learn--and that's exactly what keeps me coming back.”
