  - question: |
      Max, what's your Jewish story?
    answer: |
      I am an Ashkenazi Jew, my mother is Polish/Russian/Austrian/Ukranian and my father is Russian/Ukrainian.  My grandmother's grandfather was a rabbi in Mariupol (in Ukraine).  Both sides of my family emigrated to America to escape the pogroms.  I was raised in a Conservative Jewish household, I was bar-mitzvahed, was involved with BBYO and went on Birthright Israel.  My family is now becoming more Reform but I have become increasingly more religious.  I went to Camp Nageela in the Catskills, an Orthodox camp, for 10 years.  I'm involved with Hillel on campus and also JLA out of Boro Park.

      There's an interesting story whereby my family was saved by the Japanese Empire.  My grandmother's mother was a child, on a train in Russia during the war and a Russian soldier discovered that she was Jewish and wanted to kill her.  A Japanese soldier, also on the train, realized what was going on and threatened to kill the train conductor if they would throw the child off the train.

      "But this is a Jew," said the Russian soldier, as if to say, "there is nothing wrong with killing her."

      The Japanese soldier said, "but that's a kid."

      My grandmother's mother's life was spared.  I wouldn't have existed were it not for that Japanese soldier.

  - question: |
      Tell us about your experience with TPF:
    answer: |
      My mentor is Rabbi Yossi Korngold (Director of IGNITE Outreach of Long Island).  I think he's a PHENOMENAL rabbi.  PHENOMENAL. Even though I was offered 15 bucks, I was a little nervous at first.  I didn't know what the subject matter would be.  After listening to the podcasts, I realized that they're very entertaining.  They're a very diverse set of topics.

      Overall, an 11 out of 10; just a really well-done process.

  - question: |
      Where do you stand in your Jewish growth?
    answer: |
      Rabbi Yossi Korngold told me a very interesting quote that changed my life within Judaism.  When I told him I'm not really an observant Jew but I'm interested in keeping a few things, like saying Modeh Ani and Sh'ma and Kiddush on Shabbos, he said,

      "OK, those are great things!  Let's say someone found a diamond necklace on the side of the road and took it to pawn shop.  The owners of the pawn shop say, 'The diamonds are real.  The only problem is that there are settings for 75 diamonds and 10 of them are missing.  Do you want us to throw it out?'  You would think to yourself, 'What, are you crazy?  You want to throw out a diamond necklace?  There are 65 diamonds on there, why would you throw it out?'"

      Rabbi Yossi equated the diamonds to mitzvot and said that if you do some mitzvot, it's better than none, and each one is like a diamond.  So since I started TPF I say Modeh Ani every morning, I have it written on my wall, in my dorm room, in my mom's house and my dad's house (because my parents are separated).  Same with Sh'ma.  I have a Kiddush cup which I didn't take to the mikveh but did take to the ocean, I occasionally do Kiddush with my yarmulke.  But mainly Modeh Ani and Sh'ma--praying every single day, and it's because of the Fellowship.  I feel very connected to G-d and I think I understand life just a little bit better because of this, overall.  How could you not?
