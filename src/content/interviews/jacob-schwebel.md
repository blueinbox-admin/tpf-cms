  - question: |
      Jacob, I heard you started keeping Shabbos.  Can you tell us about that journey?
    answer: |
      The journey towards keeping Shabbos started when I was doing Shabbat dinners with friends I made during a recent summer program in Tel Aviv.  Everyone would bring dishes to make a full Shabbos meal.  Every Shabbos after that, I got more and more into obeying the Shabbos laws.  My first major Shabbos kept was when I stayed at Heritage House in Jerusalem.  We were invited to a huge, beautiful Shabbos lunch with the Claman Family in the Old City, followed by hours of powerful davening out loud. I felt the holiness of Shabbos and it made me feel great.

      When I came back to Georgia, I learned how to make challah and would make two loaves every Shabbat as well as light Shabbat candles.

      Now I go to all Shabbat services at Georgia Tech Chabad.  It's about an hour away from my campus, but well worth the trip. I also keep my phone off for the full Shabbos.

      The main challenge to keeping Shabbos is being in a fraternity.  Learning how to balance the two with time management was a big step, but Shabbos comes first.

  - question: |
      Tell us about your experience with TPF.
    answer: |
      My experience with TPF has been amazing. Rabbi Netanel encouraged me to do the program and I wish I had started it earlier. Initially, I was nervous to do an interview to get accepted to the program because my background in Judaism is not very strong. But I am always open to learning.

      Once a week, Rabbi Netanel will send me a podcast to listen to. I will listen to it with open ears and take notes. Even the small details that the Rabbis are talking about are facts I never knew about so I’m engaged the whole podcast.

      When I meet with Rabbi Netanel over Zoom, we discuss the podcast in depth, and he answers any questions I have about the podcast, or other topics relating to Judaism. It’s amazing having a mentor that is always open to questions. Some weeks there will be more than 1 student on the call, which gives me insight into how others relate to the podcast of the week.
  - question: |
      Do you prefer the privacy/personal attention of individual podcast discussions, or do prefer the group setting?

      I prefer group calls because I am able to see how other Jews at bigger universities and living different lives relate to the same topics. When we talk about antisemitism, I can’t relate that much, but those who attend bigger colleges can, and through that I’m able to see what they are dealing with.
