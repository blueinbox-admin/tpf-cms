  - question: |
      Noah, can you tell us your family's Jewish story?
    answer: |
      All of my grandparents were born in the US; 3 out of 4 were first-generation Americans whose parents came over from Russia, Ukraine and Romania, presumably fleeing pogroms or antisemitism of some sort.  I unfortunately don't have too many stories of my relatives' lives in the 'old country.'
  - question: |
      What kind of Jewish home did you grow up in, and what kind of Jewish education did you receive?
    answer: |
      I was raised in a conservative Jewish household, more traditional than religious.  I went to Jewish summer camp every year, went to Israel on a Birthright-esque trip, had a bar mitzvah, and celebrated the major holidays.  We ate shrimp but not pork.  I went to public school, Sunday school from K-12 and Hebrew school twice a week from 3rd grade up to bar mitzvah.  If we went to synagogue outside of the High Holidays, it was the obligatory 1x/month sessions one had to do to get 'bar mitzva'ed.'  I wasn't necessarily interested or expected to bring what I learned in religious school into the otherwise secular part of my life.
  - question: |
      How did you hear about The Podcast Fellowship?
    answer: |
      Through my amazing mentor Kayla Soroka (of The Cincinnati Jewish Experience).  She holds awesome educational events in-person, independent of The Podcast Fellowship.  Her programming is the first Jewish education I've regularly participated in as an adult.  Kayla brought TPF up at one of her events, and a guy named Michael Evers and I were the two that took her up on it.
  - question: |
      Would you have been interested in TPF without the $15 stipend offer?

      I would not have been interested in TPF without the stipend.  That being said, if this is me 'getting paid for my work,' then this is the best job ever.
  - question: |
      Tell us about your experience with TPF.
    answer: |
      Kayla, Michael and I usually round-robin, taking turns recommending what to listen to for the next week.  It is incredibly common that our conversations, in-person or on the phone, hit the 2-hour mark.  We get carried away.

      Often for me, there's some terminology in the podcast that I didn't understand; Kayla educates me on it.

      In terms of preparing for our discussions, I usually listen to the podcast and note--mentally or on my iPhone notes--what I thought was especially poignant or confusing.  The co-creation usually starts with just anyone chiming in on what they thought was significant.
  - question: |

  - question: |
      Where do you stand in your Jewish observance today?
    answer: |
      I am similar in Jewish observance to as I was growing up although I am trying to be more strict on not eating treyf and celebrating more of the minor fast holidays.
  - question: |
      Have you experienced increased anti-Semitism since October 7, 2023?
    answer: |
      I have spoken with friends who are more critical of Israel than I would be, and contextualize the Oct. 7th attacks in a way I disagree with, but I haven't had any interactions related to the current war that I would characterize as anti-Semitic.  My only personal experience with ant-Semitism has been with coworkers who thought Jews were controlling the world---probably along the lines of The Protocols of the Elders of Zion.  When I disagreed with them and they told me to 'do my research,' I informed them that I was in fact a member of the group which they were talking about.  They were not phased, embarrassed or caught off-guard.  The response was, of course, 'oh yeah, but you're one of the good ones.'  It is possible to be insulted by a compliment!

      In general, I actually find it incredibly important to talk to people who are anti-Semitic (and find it unproductive to call them that).  I don't shy away from this; we need to humanize ourselves to them.  If they 'unknowingly' befriend a Jew, maybe the knowledge of this after the fact makes them rethink things, making us less of a caricature/fairy tale in their eyes.
